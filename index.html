About

Nicholas Maistros is a fiction writer whose work has been published in The Literary Review, Bellingham Review, Beloit Fiction Journal, Nimrod International Journal of Prose and Poetry, Sycamore Review, Sequestrum, Washington Square, and Witness. His work has also earned finalist positions in contests run by Gulf Coast and Glimmer Train.

He holds an MFA in creative writing from Colorado State University, where he taught courses in literature, composition, drama, and creative writing. He has served as an assistant editor for Colorado Review and co-editor for the new literary journal Palooka. His book reviews can be found in Colorado Review.

Nicholas works for the International Center for Transitional Justice, a human rights NGO in New York City, and volunteers as a writer and communications coordinator for the Academy of Medical & Public Health Services, a non-profit organization that brings free health services to Brooklyn's immigrant populations.

He currently lives and writes in Seattle, Washington.

These Imaginary Acts: a Novel

These Imaginary Acts is the story of Raymond Meeks, whose life did not turn out the way hed hoped. After a series of family tragedies, Raymond dropped out of high school and left his hometown in Ohio with vague aspirations of becoming a filmmaker. Fifteen years later, he's no closer to Hollywood. He's just lost his job editing TV promos. He sold his car to make rent. He's alone and purposeless. So he calls his old friend, Kelly, for help.

Kelly Suthpin had been meant for great things, when he was seventeen. He was charismatic, handsome, envied. His mother shouted his name at diving competitions, certain that his Olympic prowess would bring them a magnificent future. But now, living in Kelly's spare bedroom, Raymond is having a difficult time recognizing the boy he'd once adored. This Kelly has gained weight. He works at the pharmacy of a noisy supermarket chain. He blows off steam at a gun range. He fights with his wife, Dee, who Raymond begins to suspect is just as disenchanted with the person Kelly has become.

Raymond soon finds himself competing with Kelly, in ways that exceedingly risk their lifelong friendship. And as the cracks in Kelly's marriage deepen, Raymond decides that Dee has given up far too much to stay trapped in this small town. Dee needs a new life. She needs a partner who's adventurous, daring—the man Raymond is now determined to be.

The novel also blends elements of historical fiction as Raymond, having found a job projecting OMNIMAX movies at the Cincinnati Museum, becomes fascinated with the subject of its latest film—Major A. B. Rogers, the eccentric nineteenth-century surveyor who discovered a deadly mountain pass in the Canadian Rockies, nearly going mad in the process. As he narrates Rogers's incredible story, Raymond decides that greatness does not have to contain itself in the postcard frames of history. Mountains can still be conquered. Tracks can still be laid. And if Kelly has lost sight of his own ambitions, maybe it's time for Raymond Meeks to take his place, in whatever way he can.

An excerpt from These Imaginary Acts has been published in Washington Square's Winter/Spring 2015 issue.
